Plate 07 · Part III — Training

Pretraining at Scale

The knowledge inside an LLM was not programmed — it was compressed from trillions of tokens of human text by one absurdly simple objective: predict the next word. This plate explains how that works, what it costs, and why a 2022 paper from DeepMind rewrote the playbook for how to spend a compute budget.

Reading time · 20 min Prereqs · Plate 06 Code · PyTorch · transformers

The deceptively simple objective

Pretraining an LLM uses a single objective: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. That's it. No labels, no human annotation, no task definition. The text itself provides the supervision — the "correct answer" at every position is simply the token that actually comes next in the document.

This is called self-supervised learning. The training corpus is billions of documents; each document is sliced into fixed-length windows (the context window size, e.g. 4,096 tokens); and for every window, the model is asked to produce a probability distribution over the vocabulary at each position. The label at position i is token i + 1.

It sounds almost too simple to work. Why would predicting the next word teach a model to write code, answer medical questions, or reason through logic puzzles? The answer, first articulated for language models by OpenAI's GPT-2 paper, is that next-token prediction is a proxy for understanding. To consistently predict the next word in a Wikipedia article about quantum mechanics, you have to have encoded something about quantum mechanics. To predict the next line of Python, you need to have encoded something about Python semantics. The objective forces the model to compress the statistical regularities of an enormous corpus — and those regularities are, to a first approximation, knowledge.

Analogy · web-scale fill-in-the-blank

Imagine an intern who learns to code by reading every Stack Overflow post and GitHub repository ever written, with no teacher — just trying to predict the next character at every step. If they're good enough at that game, they'll have internalized the idioms, the API names, the common bugs. Compression ≈ understanding (within the limits of the training distribution). Pretraining is that game, played on the entire web, in every language, across every domain, simultaneously.

Where the data comes from (and why cleaning matters)

The scale of pretraining data is hard to intuit. GPT-3 was trained on ~300 B tokens; modern models train on 1–15 trillion tokens. The primary sources:

  • Common Crawl. A non-profit crawl of a large fraction of the web, released monthly. Enormous (petabytes) and messy — raw HTML, spam, duplicates, and adult content intermixed with genuinely useful text.
  • The Pile (EleutherAI). A curated 825 GB dataset combining Common Crawl subsets, books, arXiv, GitHub, Wikipedia, StackExchange, and more, with intentional diversity.
  • FineWeb (Hugging Face). A newer 15 T-token filtered Common Crawl derivative that prioritises quality over size — a strong open alternative to proprietary training sets.
  • Books, Wikipedia, code, academic papers. High signal-to-noise sources given outsized weight relative to raw web text.

Raw Common Crawl is nearly unusable without cleaning. A standard pipeline includes: deduplication (MinHash LSH over n-grams to find near-duplicates at the document and paragraph level), language detection, heuristic quality filters (remove documents where most content is punctuation, URLs, or boilerplate), and toxicity/spam filtering. Deduplication is critical: memorised near-duplicates degrade generalisation, inflate apparent performance, and raise copyright risks.

Gotcha · data quality and contamination

The #1 hidden variable in pretraining is data quality, not model size. Models trained on heavily filtered corpora often outperform larger models on noisier data. A related pitfall: benchmark contamination — if MMLU, HumanEval, or GSM8K questions appeared in your training data, your reported scores are inflated. Serious labs deduplicate against known benchmarks; many don't. Reading model papers critically means asking: "what did they actually train on, and did they check for contamination?"

The cost: compute, GPUs, dollars

Pretraining is expensive in a way that's qualitatively different from anything else in software engineering. A commodity web service at scale costs thousands per month; pretraining a frontier model costs tens of millions of dollars for a single run.

The standard unit is the FLOP (floating-point operation). Counting the FLOPs for one forward-backward pass through an N-parameter model on a D-token dataset is approximately 6ND — the factor of 6 accounts for the forward pass (2N), the backward pass (4N for gradients of weights and activations). Training GPT-3 (175 B params, 300 B tokens) required roughly 3 × 10²³ FLOPs. At H100 peak throughput (~1,000 TFLOP/s, with ~40% utilisation in practice), that's around 9,000 GPU-days, or about 25 GPU-years.

Practical pretraining runs use hundreds to thousands of GPUs in parallel for weeks to months. A70B model from scratch is a $1–10 M exercise at current cloud prices — before the engineering time, experimentation, and ablations. This is why checkpoints are sacred: losing a run to a hardware fault or a misconfigured hyperparameter without a recent checkpoint is measured in dollars and weeks.

Loss, perplexity, and what "learning" looks like

The training objective is cross-entropy loss — the negative log-likelihood of the correct next token under the model's distribution. Lower loss means the model is assigning higher probability to the correct tokens.

L = −(1/D) Σt log P(tokent | token1..t-1)

The twin metric reported in papers is perplexity: ppl = exp(L). Perplexity has a pleasing interpretation: a perplexity of 10 means the model is, on average, as uncertain as if it had to choose uniformly among 10 equally likely tokens. A random baseline on a 32,000-token vocabulary would have perplexity ~32,000. A well-trained LLM achieves perplexity in the low single digits on held-out web text — meaning it's usually assigning high confidence to the right answer.

python · cross-entropy next-token loss 
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
import math

# Illustrative: measure loss on a short text snippet
model_id = "HuggingFaceTB/SmolLM2-135M"
tok   = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_id)
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_id, torch_dtype=torch.float32)
model.eval()

text = "The capital of France is Paris and it is known for the Eiffel Tower."
ids  = tok(text, return_tensors="pt").input_ids   # shape: (1, seq_len)

with torch.no_grad():
    outputs = model(ids)                           # logits: (1, seq_len, vocab_size)
    logits  = outputs.logits

# Next-token prediction: input[i] predicts target[i+1]
# Shift: logits[0, :-1] vs labels ids[0, 1:]
shift_logits = logits[0, :-1, :]                  # (seq_len-1, vocab_size)
shift_labels = ids[0, 1:]                         # (seq_len-1,)

loss = F.cross_entropy(shift_logits, shift_labels)
ppl  = math.exp(loss.item())

print(f"Cross-entropy loss : {loss.item():.4f}")
print(f"Perplexity         : {ppl:.2f}")
# A well-trained model on predictable text gets ppl close to 1–10.
# A random 32k-vocab baseline would give ppl ≈ 32,000.
output
Cross-entropy loss : 1.8321
Perplexity         : 6.25
# The 135M model is fairly uncertain on this sentence — a larger model would be ~2–4

During training, loss decreases smoothly — at first steeply (the model quickly learns common patterns), then gradually. A spike in training loss usually signals a corrupted data batch or a numerical instability (gradient explosion), not a meaningful fluctuation. Smart training pipelines log loss with exponential moving averages and checkpoint before and after any anomaly.

Scaling laws and the Chinchilla correction

In 2020, Kaplan et al. at OpenAI published a landmark study showing that language model loss follows a power law in model size, data size, and compute budget — independently. Loss decreases predictably as you add parameters, add data, or add compute, with each axis following its own smooth curve. This was transformative: it meant you could predict how good a model would be before training it, given only your compute budget.

The practical upshot of the Kaplan scaling laws was: for a fixed compute budget, make the model as large as possible. The community followed this advice. GPT-3 at 175 B was trained on "only" 300 B tokens — far fewer than what would maximally utilise a model of that size according to the power law.

What didn't — pre-Chinchilla over-sizing

Between 2020 and 2022, the race to largest parameters produced a parade of compute-inefficient models. GPT-3, Gopher, Megatron-Turing — all trained models with many more parameters than their data budgets could fully utilise. They were good, but they were leaving performance on the table: a smaller model trained on more data for the same FLOPs would have done better. The field was optimising the wrong axis.

In 2022, Hoffmann et al. at DeepMind ran a massive set of ablations — training over 400 models at varying parameter counts and token counts across a range of compute budgets — and derived a revised scaling law. Their conclusion is now called the Chinchilla result (after the model in the paper): to minimise loss for a given compute budget C, you should scale parameters N and data tokens D equally. Specifically, the optimal ratio is roughly 20 tokens per parameter. For a 7 B parameter model, the optimal training set is ~140 B tokens, not 300 B or 1 T.

The practical impact was immediate: the 2022 Chinchilla model (70 B params, 1.4 T tokens) outperformed Gopher (280 B params) on most benchmarks despite being four times smaller — because Gopher was dramatically undertrained relative to its parameter count. Every major model released after 2023 — Llama 1, Mistral, Llama 2, Llama 3, Gemma — explicitly applies the Chinchilla result, and most push beyond it (Llama 3 at 8 B trained on 15 T tokens, far past the Chinchilla-optimal, to produce better inference-time performance on fixed hardware).

▸ The Kaplan and Chinchilla power laws, in numbers deep dive

Kaplan et al. fit: L(N, D) ≈ A/N^α + B/D^β + L∞ where α ≈ 0.076, β ≈ 0.095 — parameters decrease loss at similar rates. Under Chinchilla's revised fit, the optimal compute allocation gives Nopt ∝ C^{0.5} and Dopt ∝ C^{0.5} — equal scaling. The key intuition: once you've shown the model every token in your dataset 1× (roughly), it has extracted as much signal as a single pass can give. Training longer on the same data yields diminishing returns compared to new data. This is also why data deduplication matters so much — training on duplicates is wasted compute.

Emergence, and base vs instruct

One of the most surprising findings from scaling is emergence: abilities that are absent at small scale and appear — apparently discontinuously — at larger scale. Tasks like multi-step arithmetic, logical reasoning, and certain translation pairs show near-zero performance below a threshold model size, then jump sharply. This has been partially explained as an artefact of the metric used (log-probability loss scales smoothly; accuracy on a fixed test does not) — but whether emergence is "real" or metric-artefact, the practical consequence is the same: you can't safely predict all capabilities from interpolation alone.

After pretraining, what you have is a base model — also called a "pretrained" or "foundation" model. It is a remarkably good next-token predictor. Given "The capital of France is", it will confidently complete the sentence. Given "Write a poem about autumn," it might write a poem — or might continue in the style of an essay about poetry, or generate a Wikipedia-style article header. It doesn't know it's a chatbot. It doesn't follow instructions. It has no sense of self.

A base model is the raw material. To get a model that follows instructions, holds a conversation, and refuses harmful requests, you need alignment training — supervised fine-tuning on instruction examples, followed by reinforcement learning from human feedback (Plates 09 and 11). The resulting instruct model (also called a "chat model" or "aligned model") is what the public interacts with. Think of the base model as a very well-read intern who has read everything but been given no instructions; the instruct model is that same intern after an intensive onboarding that teaches them what the job actually requires.

Understanding the base/instruct distinction matters for practitioners: fine-tuning an instruct model for a new task sometimes hurts because it overwrites alignment; fine-tuning a base model gives more control but requires your own alignment pass. The distillation path — using a strong instruct model to generate training data for a smaller one — is now the dominant way to produce capable small open models cheaply (Plate 09).

Recap

  • Pretraining objective: predict the next token. The labels are the text itself — no annotation required.
  • Data: trillions of tokens from Common Crawl and curated corpora. Cleaning, deduplication, and quality filtering matter as much as quantity.
  • Cost: millions of dollars, thousands of GPU-days, weeks of wallclock time. Checkpoints are sacred.
  • Loss = cross-entropy; perplexity = exp(loss). A random baseline on a 32k-vocab gives ppl ≈ 32,000; a good model gets single digits.
  • Kaplan scaling laws: loss is a power law in params, data, and compute — independently predictable.
  • Chinchilla correction (Hoffmann 2022): scale params and data equally (~20 tokens/param). Pre-Chinchilla models were dramatically undertrained; every modern open model applies this.
  • The output of pretraining is a base model. Alignment training (SFT + RLHF/DPO) turns it into an instruct model.

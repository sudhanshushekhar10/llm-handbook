Fine-tuning: The Big Picture

Before you touch a training script, there is a decision to make, and getting it wrong wastes weeks. Four levers can change a model's behavior: prompting, RAG, fine-tuning, and pretraining. Each costs a different amount of money, time, and expertise. This plate is the map.

Reading time · 18 min Prereqs · Plate 06 Code · trl · transformers

Four ways to change a model's behavior

A base language model is an extraordinary general engine; it has compressed an enormous slice of human knowledge into its weights. But "general" doesn't always mean "useful for your task right now." You may need it to answer only about your product's documentation, produce JSON in a specific schema, speak in a certain persona, or handle a domain it has barely seen. There are four distinct levers you can pull, in roughly ascending order of effort and permanence.

Lever 1: Prompting. Write better instructions at runtime. A few examples in the context window (zero-shot, few-shot) can push a capable model surprisingly far. Cost: engineering hours. Change is ephemeral: it vanishes when the context ends.

Lever 2: RAG. Retrieval-Augmented Generation leaves the model weights untouched. Instead, you retrieve relevant documents at query time and paste them into the prompt. The model reasons over the retrieved text rather than recalling facts from its parameters. Fresh facts, no training required.

Lever 3: Fine-tuning. Fine-tuning resumes training on your own curated dataset to bake new behavior into the weights. This changes what the model knows how to do: its style, format, or skill profile. It does not reliably inject factual knowledge that can change over time (use RAG for that).

Lever 4: (Re)pretraining. Train a model from scratch, or continue pretraining on a large domain corpus (e.g., all of PubMed). This is the nuclear option: millions of dollars in compute, weeks of wall time, a team of specialists. Almost nobody outside a foundation-model lab does this for production.

Analogy · source code vs reference manual vs asking nicely

Fine-tuning changes the source code. The behavior is compiled into the binary: permanent, fast at inference, but costly to update. RAG hands the model a reference manual at runtime. The manual can be swapped, updated, and versioned without touching the model at all. Prompting just asks nicely. It costs nothing to try, but the model has to already know how to do the thing; you're just reminding it.

Prompt vs RAG vs fine-tune vs pretrain: a decision guide

The right tool depends on your specific need. Here is a compact decision table. Start from the top; the first row that matches your situation is usually your answer.

Need Recommended approach Why
Fresh or frequently changing facts (today's prices, live docs, private knowledge base) RAG Facts live outside the weights, so they can be updated without retraining. Fine-tuning on facts leads to hallucinations and staleness.
New output format or schema (always reply as JSON, follow a specific template) Fine-tune or structured-output decoding A few hundred examples teaching the format beats fragile prompt engineering. Structured-output libraries can enforce schema without training.
New skill or domain style (write SQL like a DBA, summarize like a radiologist, code in an internal DSL) Fine-tune (SFT) Skills are behavioral; they belong in the weights. Prompting helps at the margins; fine-tuning internalizes the style robustly.
Private data the model must not reveal (train on PII but never expose it) Be very careful: prefer RAG with access control Fine-tuning memorizes training data; extraction attacks can surface it. Access-controlled RAG keeps private data outside the weights entirely.
Lowest inference cost / smallest latency Fine-tune a small model A fine-tuned 3B model can outperform a prompted 70B on narrow tasks at a fraction of the serving cost.
Entirely new language or domain vocabulary (novel scientific corpus, code in a new language) Continued pretraining + fine-tune If the base model has barely seen the domain, SFT has nothing to build on. Continued pretraining first gives the model the raw vocabulary, then SFT shapes behavior.
Capability is already there, model just ignores instructions Prompt engineering first; few-shot examples Base models are capable but unfocused. A good system prompt and a handful of examples may be all you need. Try before spending on training.
Gotcha · don't fine-tune to inject facts

The most common fine-tuning mistake: trying to teach the model new factual knowledge by putting facts in training examples. Models fine-tuned this way hallucinate more, not less; the model learns the format of a confident answer while the underlying fact retrieval is still unreliable. If the information changes (prices, names, dates), you'll be constantly retraining. Use RAG for facts; use fine-tuning for skills and style.

Supervised fine-tuning and instruction tuning

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is the most common form of fine-tuning. You assemble a dataset of (input, ideal output) pairs and continue training the model on them: same cross-entropy loss used in pretraining, but on your curated data instead of the raw web. The model learns to produce outputs that look like the examples in your dataset.

Instruction tuning is SFT applied to a particular dataset shape: instructions paired with helpful responses. The InstructGPT paper from OpenAI in 2022 crystallized this idea: a base GPT-3 model, fine-tuned on 13,000 instruction-response pairs from human annotators, became dramatically more useful as a general assistant. This is how most "chat" or "instruct" variants of open models are created.

One practical detail that trips up almost every engineer building their first fine-tune: the chat template. Chat models expect input formatted with special tokens marking turn boundaries (e.g., <|user|>, <|assistant|>, or the Llama-specific [INST] syntax). If you format your training data differently from how the model was originally fine-tuned, the fine-tune silently fails. Plate 12 covers this in depth.

After SFT, many models go through a second stage of alignment training: teaching them to be helpful, harmless, and honest. That's the subject of Plate 11. For now, think of SFT as the first and most fundamental step.

From a software-eng POV

SFT is essentially supervised learning with a language model loss; it's logistic regression on tokens, not a mysterious ritual. Your training loop looks like: format examples into input-output pairs, tokenize them, run a forward pass, compute cross-entropy loss on the output tokens only (mask the input/prompt tokens so you don't "teach" the model its own prompt), backpropagate, update weights. The trl library's SFTTrainer wraps this boilerplate so you hand it a dataset and a model and it does the masking automatically.

Full fine-tuning vs PEFT

Once you've decided to fine-tune, you face a second decision: which parameters do you update?

Full fine-tuning updates every weight in the model. For a 7B-parameter model using the AdamW optimizer, that means storing gradients and optimizer states alongside the weights: roughly 16 bytes per parameter, or over 100 GB of GPU memory. On a single A100 (80 GB VRAM), a 7B full fine-tune is essentially impossible without multiple GPUs and gradient checkpointing tricks. For a 70B model, you're looking at a small cluster.

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) is the family of techniques that bypass this problem by freezing most of the model and training only a small set of additional parameters. The flagship approach is LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation); it represents weight updates as products of two tiny matrices and adds them on top of the frozen weights. The resulting "adapter" is typically 1–2% the size of the base model. The earlier adapter approach inserted small bottleneck modules between layers; LoRA is more elegant and has largely superseded it.

Plate 10 covers LoRA mechanics in full, including the math behind low-rank decomposition, the rank and alpha hyperparameters, which layers to target, and QLoRA (LoRA on a 4-bit quantized base). If you're planning to run a fine-tune on a single GPU, go read Plate 10 next. It is the practical how-to.

Pitfalls: forgetting, overfitting, the alignment tax

Fine-tuning is powerful but has a trio of failure modes worth understanding before you write a single line of training code.

Catastrophic forgetting. When you fine-tune on a narrow dataset, the model gradient-descends toward your examples and away from everything it learned during pretraining. This can degrade performance on tasks unrelated to your training data: the model "forgets" its general capabilities. The severity depends on how far your dataset is from the pretraining distribution and how many steps you train. PEFT methods like LoRA largely mitigate this because the frozen weights retain all original knowledge; only the small adapter changes.

Overfitting on small data. A 7B-parameter model has an enormous capacity to memorize. If you fine-tune on only a few hundred examples, it can overfit in just a few epochs, achieving near-zero training loss while performing poorly on new inputs. Signs: the model starts repeating training examples verbatim, or its outputs collapse to a narrow set of patterns. Mitigations: early stopping on a held-out validation set, regularization, distillation (generating synthetic data from a larger model to augment your dataset), and using PEFT (fewer trainable parameters = lower overfitting risk).

The alignment tax. This one is subtler. When you fine-tune a model that went through alignment training (RLHF, DPO; Plate 11), your SFT may partially undo the safety and helpfulness behaviors that alignment baked in. This is sometimes called the "alignment tax": the behavioral guardrails cost some task-specific capability, and re-tuning for capability can cost some of the guardrails. There's no universal fix: the typical approach is to either start from a base (pre-alignment) model and do your own alignment after SFT, or to fine-tune carefully on a small dataset for minimal epochs to preserve alignment.

A minimal SFT example

Below is a real, runnable SFT setup using the trl library's SFTTrainer. The Local tab uses a tiny model (GPT-2) so it runs on CPU/MPS. The Cloud tab uses Mistral-7B on a CUDA GPU, which is the practical minimum for production-quality results. The actual LoRA configuration details (rank, alpha, target_modules) are covered in Plate 10; here we just show the SFT training loop structure.

python · SFT with trl 
# pip install trl transformers datasets accelerate
from datasets import Dataset
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForCausalLM
from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig

# --- 1. Tiny dataset of instruction-response pairs ---
data = [
    {"text": "<|user|>\nWhat is the capital of France?\n<|assistant|>\nParis."},
    {"text": "<|user|>\nExplain REST in one sentence.\n<|assistant|>\nREST is a stateless HTTP convention where resources are identified by URLs and manipulated with standard verbs (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE)."},
    {"text": "<|user|>\nWrite a Python function to reverse a string.\n<|assistant|>\ndef reverse(s: str) -> str:\n    return s[::-1]"},
]
dataset = Dataset.from_list(data)

# --- 2. Load model (tiny, runs on CPU/MPS) ---
model_id = "gpt2"
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_id)
tokenizer.pad_token = tokenizer.eos_token
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_id)

# --- 3. Configure SFT ---
config = SFTConfig(
    output_dir="./sft-gpt2",
    num_train_epochs=3,
    per_device_train_batch_size=2,
    learning_rate=2e-5,
    logging_steps=10,
    max_seq_length=256,
)

trainer = SFTTrainer(
    model=model,
    args=config,
    train_dataset=dataset,
    processing_class=tokenizer,
)

# --- 4. Train ---
trainer.train()
print("Training complete. Model saved to ./sft-gpt2")

# Requires: ~16 GB VRAM for fp16, or ~8 GB with QLoRA (see Plate 10)
from datasets import load_dataset
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForCausalLM, BitsAndBytesConfig
from peft import LoraConfig
from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig
import torch

# --- 1. Load a real instruction dataset ---
dataset = load_dataset("HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat_200k", split="train_sft[:5000]")

# --- 2. Load Mistral-7B in 4-bit (QLoRA) ---
model_id = "mistralai/Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3"
bnb_config = BitsAndBytesConfig(
    load_in_4bit=True,
    bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
)
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_id)
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    model_id,
    quantization_config=bnb_config,
    device_map="auto",
)

# --- 3. LoRA adapter config (see Plate 10 for tuning these) ---
peft_config = LoraConfig(
    r=16,
    lora_alpha=32,
    target_modules=["q_proj", "v_proj"],
    lora_dropout=0.05,
    bias="none",
    task_type="CAUSAL_LM",
)

# --- 4. SFT config ---
config = SFTConfig(
    output_dir="./sft-mistral-7b",
    num_train_epochs=1,
    per_device_train_batch_size=4,
    gradient_accumulation_steps=4,
    learning_rate=2e-4,
    bf16=True,
    logging_steps=25,
    max_seq_length=1024,
)

trainer = SFTTrainer(
    model=model,
    args=config,
    train_dataset=dataset,
    peft_config=peft_config,
    processing_class=tokenizer,
)

# --- 5. Train ---
trainer.train()
trainer.save_model("./sft-mistral-7b")

Recap

  • Four levers adapt model behavior: prompting (fastest, no persistence), RAG (swappable knowledge, no training), fine-tuning (bakes skills and style into weights), and pretraining (nuclear option for entirely new domains).
  • Use RAG for facts that change; use fine-tuning for behaviors and skills that should be consistent. Never fine-tune to inject volatile knowledge.
  • SFT is fine-tuning on (input, output) pairs with a standard cross-entropy loss; instruction tuning is SFT on instruction-response data specifically.
  • Full fine-tuning costs ~16 bytes/param of memory, prohibitive for large models on a single GPU. PEFT (especially LoRA) freezes the base and trains a tiny adapter, cutting memory by 10–20×.
  • Watch for catastrophic forgetting (narrow SFT erases general capabilities), overfitting (too few examples, too many epochs), and the alignment tax (fine-tuning can undo safety behaviors).

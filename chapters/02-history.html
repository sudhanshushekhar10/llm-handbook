A Short History of Language Models

Every quirk in modern LLMs — the context window, the token, the attention mechanism — was an answer to a specific failure. Knowing what broke and why is the fastest way to understand why things work the way they do today.

Reading time · 15 min Prereqs · Plate 01 Code · None

Counting words: n-grams and their ceiling

The first formal treatment of language as a probabilistic system came from Claude Shannon in 1948. Shannon asked a deceptively simple question: how much information is in a piece of text? To answer it, he modelled language as a process that produces one symbol at a time, where each symbol's probability depends on what came before. He called the resulting measure entropy, and the word "perplexity" — how surprised the model is by real text — still measures language model quality today.

The practical tool that followed was the n-gram model. Count how often each sequence of n words appears in a large corpus. To predict the next word, look up the last n−1 words in your frequency table and return the most common completion. A trigram model predicts word three given words one and two. It is, in essence, a giant hashmap: {"the cat": {"sat": 0.4, "ate": 0.2, "ran": 0.15, …}}.

What didn't work — and why

N-gram models hit a wall called the curse of dimensionality. To look up a 5-gram you need that exact 5-word sequence in your training data. Most 5-grams are never seen in training — the space of possible sequences is astronomical. You can smooth (add a small count to everything you haven't seen), but smoothing is a patch, not a solution. Long-range dependencies — "the bank I visited last Tuesday, which was beside the river, was closed" — are beyond any practical n-gram model. The method captured local word correlation but not meaning.

For two decades, n-gram models ran production systems: spell checkers, autocomplete, machine translation, speech recognition. They were good enough, and nothing better existed at scale. The ceiling was real but not immediately obvious when budgets and benchmarks were modest.

Meaning as geometry: neural LMs and word2vec

The break came in 2003. Yoshua Bengio and colleagues proposed the first neural language model: instead of looking up word sequences in a table, learn a continuous vector for each word — a point in a high-dimensional space — and train a small neural network to predict the next word using those vectors.

This idea, the word embedding, solved the sparsity problem by side-stepping it. Two words that appear in similar contexts (say, "cat" and "dog") learn similar vectors. You don't need the exact phrase "my dog ate" in training to predict what comes after "my cat ate" — the model generalizes because the vectors are geometrically close.

The idea was right but the training was slow. A decade later, Mikolov et al. found a way to train word vectors on billions of words in hours — word2vec. The result was stunning: the vectors arranged themselves so that arithmetic encoded relationships. "king" − "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen". Paris : France :: Berlin : Germany. Meaning had become geometry. The ML world's collective jaw dropped.

What won — and why

Learned embeddings replaced count-based vectors because they generalized. N-gram models were brittle lookup tables; embedding models built a geometry where unseen combinations could be reasoned about by proximity. This embedding paradigm — representing discrete symbols as dense vectors that encode semantic relationships — is now so fundamental that it's easy to forget it was once a controversial bet.

Memory in a loop: RNNs, LSTMs, seq2seq

Word2vec gave you word-level vectors, but a language model needs to process sequences. Enter the recurrent neural network (RNN): a for loop over tokens where each step updates a hidden state vector. Think of it as a register machine — one accumulator, updated at every step, carrying forward the "memory" of everything seen.

RNNs worked, but they were plagued by the vanishing gradient problem. To train via backpropagation, you multiply gradients through every step of the sequence. Multiply a number slightly less than 1 by itself a hundred times and it vanishes to zero — the signal about the beginning of a long sentence never reaches the loss. Hochreiter and Schmidhuber's LSTM (1997) fixed this with a gated architecture that learned what to remember and what to forget, but the fundamental sequential bottleneck remained.

In 2014, Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le built seq2seq: one LSTM encoded the source sentence into a single fixed vector; another decoded it into the target language. Machine translation improved dramatically. But that single vector — the bottleneck — was squeezed too hard on long sentences. The encoder had to cram an entire sentence's meaning into a fixed-size bag.

Bahdanau et al. cracked the bottleneck in 2014 with attention: instead of one fixed vector, the decoder could "look back" at every encoder state and decide which was most relevant at each decoding step. It was still bolted onto an RNN, but the idea — learn which parts of the input to pay attention to — was the seed of everything that followed.

What didn't work — the sequential bottleneck

The RNN/LSTM's fatal flaw was not memory capacity but parallelism. To compute step 500 you must first compute steps 1 through 499. Modern GPUs thrive on parallel matrix multiplies; serial dependencies starve them. You can't use your 10,000 GPU cores if step n must wait for step n−1. This is why the Transformer — which computes all positions simultaneously — was so transformative: it unlocked the full parallelism of modern hardware.

The Transformer moment (2017)

In June 2017, Vaswani et al. published Attention Is All You Need. The insight was brash and turned out to be correct: if attention was doing all the useful work in the RNN, throw away the recurrence entirely. Replace the sequential loop with a single operation that lets every token attend to every other token simultaneously. The whole sequence in parallel. One big matrix multiply.

Introduced the Transformer architecture: multi-head self-attention, positional encoding, and the encoder–decoder design. Every major LLM today is a direct descendant. See reference [9] →

The Transformer's self-attention mechanism lets each token look directly at every other token in the sequence, weighted by learned relevance scores. It's a soft, differentiable lookup table — a fuzzy hashmap where every key matches to some degree (Plate 05 digs into exactly how). Crucially, this is entirely parallel: a 512-token sequence runs in the same number of GPU steps as a 5-token sequence (modulo memory). Training time dropped from days to hours for equivalent quality.

Two roads: BERT vs GPT

The 2017 Transformer had two halves: an encoder that reads the full input bidirectionally and a decoder that generates output left-to-right. In 2018, two teams took one half each and ran.

Google's BERT kept the encoder. It was trained to predict randomly masked words — "the [MASK] sat on the mat" — which means it sees both the left and right context of every word simultaneously. BERT-style models became the gold standard for text understanding: classification, named-entity recognition, question answering. Bidirectionality is a superpower when you have the whole input and need to extract meaning from it.

OpenAI's GPT kept the decoder. It was trained on a simpler objective: predict the next token, left-to-right, without looking at the future (a causal mask). This is called autoregressive language modelling. GPT was smaller and less impressive than BERT on benchmarks — but it could generate.

What won — decoder-only

In hindsight, the generation objective was the right bet. A model trained to predict the next token on raw text — no labels, no annotation, just the internet — turns out to generalize remarkably: it can classify, translate, and summarize when simply asked. One objective, one architecture, all tasks. BERT-style encoders remain the right tool for embeddings and retrieval (see Plate 17), but the chatbots you interact with daily are all decoder-only. The simplicity of the autoregressive objective also meant it was trivially scalable: more data, more compute, longer training. BERT needed special tricks to scale; GPT just needed a bigger cluster.

Scale and the surprise of GPT-3

GPT-2 (2019) was the "too dangerous to release" model — 1.5 billion parameters, generating convincingly human text about whatever topic it was primed with. OpenAI released it in stages, nervously. In retrospect, GPT-2 was a toy.

GPT-3 dropped in 2020 with 175 billion parameters, trained on 300 billion tokens. The quantitative jump was expected. The qualitative surprise was not: emergent abilities appeared that no one had specifically trained for. Given a few examples in the prompt, the model could translate into languages it wasn't explicitly fine-tuned on, write code in a style it was shown, perform arithmetic it hadn't seen. This ability — zero-shot and few-shot generalization from context alone — was not in the spec. It fell out of scale.

The scaling laws paper by Kaplan et al. gave the theoretical spine: loss follows a predictable power law as you increase model size, dataset size, and compute. You could forecast how much better the next model would be before training it. This turned LLM development into something more like engineering and less like alchemy.

The ChatGPT inflection

GPT-3 was powerful but difficult to use. Raw completion models are not assistants — they'll continue your prompt in a direction you didn't intend, ignore your actual question, or produce harmful text. The fix, developed through 2021–2022, was RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback.

The recipe had three steps. First, collect human-written example answers and fine-tune GPT-3 on them (instruction tuning, or SFT). Second, train a reward model — a separate neural network that scores responses by human preference. Third, use reinforcement learning to update the language model so it produces responses the reward model rates highly. The resulting model — InstructGPT — followed instructions, refused harmful requests, and produced dramatically more useful outputs than raw GPT-3 with far fewer parameters.

ChatGPT launched in November 2022 as a chat interface to a GPT-3.5 model trained with this approach. One million users in five days. The world noticed.

The open-weights era

Meta's LLaMA (February 2023) changed the game again. A 65-billion-parameter model trained on publicly available data, released with weights for researchers. Within days it had leaked; within weeks the community had fine-tuned it for chat, instruction-following, code, and dozens of other tasks — without RLHF, using only a few thousand labelled examples and a technique called parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Plate 10). Alpaca, Vicuna, WizardLM, and hundreds of variants emerged in the months that followed.

The open-weights ecosystem proved something important: the expensive part of building a capable model is pretraining, not fine-tuning. Once a strong base model exists, adaptation is cheap. This has reshaped the industry: closed models compete on frontier capability; open models compete on accessibility, customizability, and privacy. Both ecosystems are vibrant and each borrows ideas from the other.

Recap

  • N-grams were the first probabilistic language models — giant hashmaps of word co-occurrence — but they couldn't generalize to unseen sequences or capture long-range meaning.
  • Neural LMs and embeddings (Bengio 2003, word2vec 2013) replaced sparse tables with dense vectors where geometry encodes meaning — solving sparsity by generalization.
  • RNNs and LSTMs introduced sequential memory but hit a wall: the serial step dependency prevents GPU parallelism, and long-range gradients vanish.
  • Bahdanau's attention (2014) let the decoder look back at all encoder states — a bolt-on fix that turned out to contain the real insight.
  • The Transformer (2017) threw away recurrence entirely: all positions attend to all positions in parallel. Training time collapsed; model quality soared.
  • BERT vs GPT split the architecture: bidirectional encoders for understanding, causal decoders for generation. Decoder-only won for chat.
  • Scale surprised everyone: emergent abilities (zero-shot task generalization) weren't programmed — they fell out of training on more data with more compute.
  • RLHF turned a powerful but unruly completion engine into a usable assistant. ChatGPT made this visible to the world.
  • Open-weights models (LLaMA, 2023) proved pretraining is the expensive step; adaptation is cheap — reshaping who can build with LLMs.
